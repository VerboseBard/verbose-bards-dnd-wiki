# Fey

The Fey are magical beings of the [Feywild](../places/feywild.md), shaped by season, philosophy, and court allegiance. Mortals often try to reduce them to simple categories, but Fey identity is layered: what looks like contradiction is usually just a different harmony of the same forces.

The [Divine Gate](divine-gate.md) changed how the Fey survive outside their own realm. The greater the Fey, the more magic they require to remain stable, sane, and powerful in the mortal world.

## Seelie and Unseelie

Seelie and Unseelie are philosophies, not neat morality labels.

- Seelie Fey value tradition, structure, and the natural order.
- Unseelie Fey value freedom, chaos, self-interest, and the delight of disruption.

Both hold oaths and contracts as sacred, but they interpret them differently.

- Seelie enforce the letter of a bargain as written.
- Unseelie search for loopholes, edge cases, and traps in wording.

Neither outlook is inherently "good" or "evil." Seelie cruelty is usually the cruelty of law, while Unseelie cruelty is usually the cruelty of intent.

## The Courts

Fey society is commonly spoken of through courts and sovereigns.

- [Summer Court](../places/summer-court.md): growth, passion, vitality, and warmth.
- Winter Court: rest, preservation, endings, and harsh truth.
- Spring Court: renewal, rebirth, and balance.
- Autumn Court: harvest, transition, and letting go.
- Court of Shadows: secrecy, hidden knowledge, and manipulation.

Bell also oversees three courts that sit slightly apart from the seasonal cycle:

- Court of Dreams: imagination, symbolism, and the space between waking and sleep.
- Court of Mischief: trickery, disruption, and chaotic change.
- Shared Spring Court: renewal and awakening, co-ruled with the Lady of the Glade.

Fey courts are not all rigid governments. Some are political, some are philosophical, and some are simply the shape a powerful Fey's influence takes in the world.

## High Fey and Lesser Fey

High Fey are the rulers, sovereigns, archfey, and ancient figures who carry the weight of courts and domains.

- They are the ones most affected by the [Divine Gate](divine-gate.md).
- They need stronger anchors, deeper bargains, and more sustained sources of magic.
- The longer they remain away from the [Feywild](../places/feywild.md), the more they risk fading or becoming desperate.

Lesser Fey are the broader population of Fey creatures.

- Brownies, pixies, dryads, satyrs, redcaps, and similar beings.
- Some are wild, some are courtly, and some are independent.
- They may be powerful in their own right, but they usually do not shape the broader politics of the [Feywild](../places/feywild.md) the way High Fey do.

## Threefold Identity

Fey are often understood through three linked aspects:

1. Season of Life
2. Philosophical Alignment
3. Court Allegiance

A Fey can be Winter-hearted, Seelie, and sworn to Summer all at once. To mortals, this seems contradictory. To the Fey, it is simply the shape of a complete being.

The three parts are distinct:

- Season of Life: the Fey's personal stage of being.
- Philosophy: Seelie or Unseelie.
- Court Allegiance: the political, social, or magical structure they serve.

These layers do not have to align.

## The Power of Three

Three is sacred to the Fey.

- Birth, death, and rebirth.
- Waxing, full, and waning moon.
- Offer, condition, and consequence in a bargain.
- Invocation, transformation, and completion in ritual magic.

Fey rituals and identities often move in threes because that structure reflects balance instead of binary choice.

## Hidden Oath and Wild Hunt

In [Moon Stone Collectors, Part 16](../sessions/moon-stone-collectors-part-16.md), [Wisdom](../people/wisdom.md) explains a guarded Fey secret because she is not herself Fey and is not bound by the same oath.

According to Wisdom, the Fey fled into the [Feywild](../places/feywild.md) after the Second-Age war with the giants and the arrival of the first dragon. At the heart of that reality is cornerstone spellwork that helps hold the realm together. Fey who come of age and learn this secret swear not to speak of it, tying their magic to that oath.

The Wild Hunt exists to enforce those oaths. Wisdom frames this as a core Fey principle: if a Fey breaks a small promise, they may break all promises. This is why [Juan](../people/juan.md)'s missing oath-memory matters. His connection to [The Weaver](the-weaver.md) is not only about stolen luck, but about a suppressed duty tied to one of the Feywild's deepest protections.

## Bargains and Survival

Because the [Divine Gate](divine-gate.md) restricts direct access to the [Feywild](../places/feywild.md), powerful Fey survive in the mortal realm by feeding on mortal life and feeling. This is not just greed. It is often a practical necessity.

They draw on:

- potential
- fame or renown
- essence, talent, youth, beauty, voice, or soul
- joy, wonder, love, hope, fear, despair, and grief

Common methods include:

- Deals and bargains
- Debt through gratitude
- Artifacts and tokens that store [Feywild](../places/feywild.md) power
- Anchors in the mortal world, such as sacred groves, ley lines, lakes, stones, and crossroads
- Feeding on emotions through performances, dreams, festivals, or engineered fear

Fey also use artifacts, anchors, debts, and oaths to root themselves in the mortal world. Gratitude can become debt. A thank you can become a hook.

High Fey who remain too long in the mortal world begin to fade, starve, or grow desperate.

## Practical Effects

When Fey remain too long away from their home power, they can:

- lose stability
- grow thin or faded in appearance
- become more manipulative or reckless
- overreach in bargains
- drain mortals too hard

In play, this means Fey aid is never free, even when it looks generous.

## Player Guide

If a Fey offers help, ask:

- what are they taking?
- what are they anchoring to?
- what emotion are they feeding on?
- what loophole might they exploit later?

## Player Handout

Fey are not just tricksters or nature spirits. They come from the [Feywild](../places/feywild.md), and each one is shaped by:

- their Season of Life
- their philosophy, Seelie or Unseelie
- their court allegiance

They love bargains, oaths, symbols, and emotional leverage. They can be kind, cruel, or both at once, and they rarely see those things as opposites.

If you deal with a Fey, be careful with your words. A promise can feed them. Gratitude can bind you. And a gift may be the beginning of a debt.
